2Components and Electronics Selection

In addition to the structural design and specifications, I also had to select a series of electronics in order to power my RC flying wing. These electronics include LiPo batteries, an Electronic Speed Controller (ESC), a brushless DC Motor, Servo Motors, a transmitter, and a receiver. During this stage, I challenged myself to learn more about each of the components in terms of storage, usage, and compatibility to ensure safety. Some examples of this include, ensuring the ESC has a higher amperage rating and built in BEC to avoid fire, checking the LiPo for safe connectors and safe discharge rate, and learning about LiPo charging safety.

Electronics used in radio-controlled flying wing. Shown are a 5000mah 80C 2S LiPo Battery, (2) 9g Micro Servos, 30A Electronic Speed Controller, A2212/6T 2200KV DC Brushless Motor, and Flysky FS-i6 Transmitter and Receiver.

Beyond compatibility and safety, ensuring the motor produced enough thrust for a reasonable thrust to weight ratio was essential to ensure flight. The 2200KV motor shown above was able to generate a 650 gram thrust on a 2C battery ensuring for a ratio greater 1:1 (thrust:weight) allowing for ease of flight. With a motor draw of 15A on average, I decided to go with a 30A ESC allowing for a higher amp rating of about 100% more, keeping the components nice and cool during flight. In order to meet the desired flight time, I picked a battery that could withstand the motor draw for a reasonable flight time. Using the following equation below with my 2200mah battery, I was able to get a flight time of about 7 minutes (perfect for a beginner RC aircraft) using only 80% discharge for LiPo battery health safety. \[Flight Time = {{\text{Battery Capacity } (Ah) \over \text{Motor Amp Draw } (A)}*{48 min \over h}}\] I then decided to look into feasible materials that would be easily accessible and easily formable. After doing some lengthy research, I found that many radio-controlled aircrafts use foam board to build the structure of the aircraft since it was so lightweight and robust. As well, repairing and replacing components in the case of damage (since it will be my first time flying something of this sort) is easy and favorable. I created a spreadsheet of the weight of the components and additional parts that might be an addition to the overall weight. An additional 150 grams was added in calculations as a weight estimate to account for small parts such as push rods, hinges, and the general lightweight structure. 2Aerodynamic Analysis

I started by calculating the Wing Cubed Loading for my design. The wing cubed loading is expressed as: \[WCL = {w \over SA^{(3/2)}}\] where \(WCL\) is the wing cubed loading, \(w\) is the weight, and \(SA\) is the wing surface area. The wing cubed loading specifies the general flying characteristics of a plane, comparing weight and surface area, where a lower \(WCL\) means slower flying. Ensuring that my \(WCL\) was about 4 was crucial since I wanted to build a slow flying wing. You can see how I got weight estimates in the previous section. Keeping this in mind, I solved for the surface area required to maintain a WCL of 4, which came to be \({0.25m^2}\). I used this to then determine combinations of span and chord. You can see a sketch of a successful combination below.

After deciding general dimensions, I went ahead to pick an airfoil. Taking into consideration that I was going to use foamboard, it was crucial for me to pick the most optimal airfoil that can be formed using a knife and foam board. This led me to narrow down my search to two airfoils; the Clark-Y airfoil and the N-10. Unfortunately, both are flat bottomed for ease of airfoil formability but are not ideal for flight trimming to ensure straight flight, but there were no other better options.

Using XFLR5 I did an aerodynamics analyis of both airfoils to better determine their flight characteristics and make an informed decision for my airfoil of use. Using the following equation (specific to aircraft flight) for Reynold's Number, I inputted a constant Reynold's Number (236878, as experienced at the cruise speed) to avoid overcomplicating calculations for my airfoil analysis. \[Re = {pvC \over {μ}}\] where \(p\) is the density of air, \(v\) is the travelling (cruise) speed, \(C\) is the mean chord length, and \(μ\) is the dynamic viscosity of air.
