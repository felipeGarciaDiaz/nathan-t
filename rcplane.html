1Engineering Design

Given that I had little prior knowledge of how flight aerodynamics and aviation actually works and the general equations involved in flight, I decided to do some research and dive deep into some textbooks and radio-controlled plane resources (More specifically, Daniel P Raymer's "Aircraft Design: A Conceptual Approach" and FliteTest). These resources helped me self learn the fundamental knowledge required to draft reasonable designs! Before diving straight into material selection and desigining my flying wing, I decided to draft a full list of criteria ranging from flying speed and time to general flying style. Here is a shortened table representing the most important criterias.

I started by calculating the Wing Cubed Loading for my design. The wing cubed loading is expressed as: \[WCL = {w \over SA^{(3/2)}}\] where \(WCL\) is the wing cubed loading, \(w\) is the weight, and \(SA\) is the wing surface area. Ensuring that my \(WCL\) was less than 4 was crucial since I wanted to build a slow flying wing. You cans ee how I got weight estimates in the next section.

2Components and Electronics Selection

In addition to the structural design and specifications, I also had to select a series of electronics in order to power my RC flying wing. These electronics include LiPo batteries, an Electronic Speed Controller (ESC), a brushless DC Motor, Servo Motors, a transmitter, and a receiver. During this stage, I challenged myself to learn more about each of the components in terms of storage, usage, and compatibility to ensure safety. Some examples of this include, ensuring the ESC has a higher amperage rating and built in BEC to avoid fire, checking the LiPo for safe connectors and safe discharge rate, and learning about LiPo charging safety.

Electronics used in radio-controlled flying wing. Shown are a 5000mah 80C 2S LiPo Battery, (2) 9g Micro Servos, 30A Electronic Speed Controller, A2212/6T 2200KV DC Brushless Motor, and Flysky FS-i6 Transmitter and Receiver.

Beyond compatibility and safety, ensuring the motor produced enough thrust for a reasonable thrust to weight ratio was essential to ensure flight. The 2200KV motor shown above was able to generate a 450 gram thrust on a 2C battery ensuring for a close to 1:1 (thrust:weight) ratio allowing for ease of flight. With a motor draw of 21.5A on average, I decided to go with a 30A ESC allowing for a higher amp rating of about 40% more, keeping the components nice and cool during flight.

I created a spread sheet of the weight of the components and additional parts that might be an addition to the overall weight. For safety measures, an additional 50 grams was added in calculations.
